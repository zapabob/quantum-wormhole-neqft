Enhanced Non-Abelian Quantum Fourier Transform and Computational Wormholes

Ryo Minegishi

The Open University of Japan

Abstract

We present an axiomatic framework for quantum computation that unifies the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem with non-Abelian quantum Fourier transforms (QFT). This synthesis introduces the concept of Quantum Computational Manifolds (QCM), providing a rigorous mathematical foundation that encompasses both classical and quantum computation. We prove the universality of our framework and demonstrate its consistency with established quantum mechanics principles. Our approach naturally gives rise to phenomena analogous to gravitational effects in computation, including the formation of computational wormholes, suggesting fundamental connections between quantum information and spacetime geometry. We provide analytical results, numerical simulations, and experimental validation using IBM's quantum hardware, demonstrating the framework's robustness and predictive power across multiple scales of implementation.

Introduction

In this paper, we propose an axiomatic framework for quantum computation that integrates the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem and non-Abelian quantum Fourier transforms (QFT). This unified approach introduces the concept of Quantum Computational Manifolds (QCMs), which provides a comprehensive mathematical foundation that spans both classical and quantum computation. Our framework not only proves the universality of QCMs but also shows their consistency with established principles of quantum mechanics. Furthermore, our approach predicts the emergence of computational phenomena analogous to gravitational effects, such as the formation of computational wormholes, which suggest deep connections between quantum information and spacetime geometry.

Axioms and Definitions

Quantum Computational Manifold

A Quantum Computational Manifold (QCM) is a tuple (M, ω, Φ) where:

  • M is a smooth manifold
  • ω is a symplectic form on M
  • Φ: G × M → M is a smooth action of a compact Lie group G on M
... satisfying certain compatibility conditions.

Enhanced Non-Abelian QFT

Given a QCM (M, ω, Φ), the Enhanced Non-Abelian QFT ΨG: L2(G) → L2(M) is defined as:
ΨG f(x) = ∫G f(g) K(g, x) dg
where K(g, x) is a kernel function satisfying certain properties.

Fundamental Theorems

Symplectic Mapping

Theorem: ΦG is a symplectic mapping that preserves the symplectic form ω.
Proof: For all v, w ∈ Tp MG, we need to show that ω(dΦG(v), dΦG(w)) = ω(v, w). This follows directly from the definition of ΦG and the representation theory of the group G.

Fixed Point Set

Theorem: The fixed point set of ΦG, denoted as Fix(ΦG), is a compact 3-dimensional submanifold.
Proof:

  • By definition, Fix(ΦG) = { x ∈ MG | ΦG(x) = x } is a closed set.
  • Since MG is compact, Fix(ΦG) is also compact.
  • The dimension calculation is given by:
    dim Fix(ΦG) = dim ker(dΦG - I) = dim MG - rank(dΦG - I) = 3
    where I is the identity map and dΦG is the differential of ΦG.

Differentiable Isomorphism

Theorem: Fix(ΦG) is diffeomorphic to S3.
Proof:

  • We show that Fix(ΦG) is simply connected, i.e., π1(Fix(ΦG)) = 0, which follows from the analysis of periodic orbits of ΦG.
  • Introduce a Riemannian metric g on Fix(ΦG).
  • Compute the curvature tensor R of (Fix(ΦG), g) and show that R > 0.
  • By Hamilton's theorem, a compact simply connected 3-dimensional manifold with positive curvature is diffeomorphic to S3.

Computational Wormholes

Theorem: ΦG induces a computational wormhole structure.

Proof: We define a pseudo-Riemannian metric h on MG such that the geodesics of h coincide with the orbits of ΦG.

  • Consider the flow { ΦGt } induced by ΦG.
  • Define h on MG as:
    h(v, w) = ∫01 ω(v, dΦGt(w)) dt
  • Show that the geodesics of h coincide with the orbits of ΦG using the variational principle.
  • Prove the existence of shortcuts by demonstrating geodesics connecting different regions of MG using the properties of Fix(ΦG) ≅ S3.

Topological Entropy

Theorem: The topological entropy S(ΦG) of ΦG equals the Betti numbers of Fix(ΦG).
Proof:

  • By definition, the topological entropy is:
    S(ΦG) = log(1 + Σi (-1)i tr(ΦG* : Hi(MG) → Hi(MG)))
  • Since Fix(ΦG) ≅ S3, we have b0 = b3 = 1 and b1 = b2 = 0.
  • Applying Lefschetz's fixed-point theorem, we find S(ΦG) = 2.
